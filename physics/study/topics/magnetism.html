Magnetism

Magnetic fields, Lorentz force, Ampère’s law, and electromagnetic induction.

1. Magnetic Field (\( \vec{B} \))

A magnetic field is a vector field that exerts magnetic forces on moving charges and magnetic dipoles. Its SI unit is the tesla (T).

\( \vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B}) \)

The direction of the force is given by the right-hand rule.

2. Biot–Savart Law

The Biot–Savart law gives the magnetic field produced by a small current element:

\( d\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{I\, d\vec{l} \times \hat{r}}{r^2} \)

Integrating over a current-carrying wire gives the total field. For a long straight conductor:

\( B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} \)

3. Ampère’s Circuital Law

The line integral of the magnetic field around a closed path equals the permeability times the current enclosed:

\( \oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}} \)

This is a fundamental principle in magnetostatics and forms part of Maxwell’s equations.

4. Lorentz Force

A charged particle moving through electric and magnetic fields experiences a combined force:

\( \vec{F} = q(\vec{E} + \vec{v} \times \vec{B}) \)

This causes particles to move in circular or helical paths, depending on their velocity relative to \( \vec{B} \).

5. Magnetic Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor

\( \vec{F} = I (\vec{L} \times \vec{B}) \)

This principle underlies electric motors, where a current in a magnetic field produces mechanical motion.

6. Electromagnetic Induction (Faraday’s Law)

A changing magnetic flux induces an electromotive force (emf):

\( \mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} \), where \( \Phi_B = \int \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{A} \)

The negative sign represents Lenz’s Law: the induced current opposes the change in flux.

Example: A coil of 100 turns encloses a magnetic flux changing at \( 0.01\, \text{Wb/s} \). Induced emf = \( \mathcal{E} = N \frac{d\Phi}{dt} = 100 \times 0.01 = 1\, \text{V} \).

7. Applications of Magnetism

  • Electric motors — convert electrical energy to mechanical motion.
  • Generators — produce electricity using changing magnetic flux.
  • Magnetic storage — hard drives and credit cards store data magnetically.
  • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) — uses strong fields for medical imaging.
  • Particle accelerators — steer charged particles using magnetic fields.

Electromagnetism

Unification of electricity and magnetism through Maxwell’s equations and electromagnetic waves.

1. Maxwell’s Equations

James Clerk Maxwell unified electric and magnetic fields into four elegant equations:

\( \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} \)  (Gauss’s Law for Electricity)
\( \nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0 \)  (Gauss’s Law for Magnetism)
\( \nabla \times \vec{E} = -\frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \)  (Faraday’s Law of Induction)
\( \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J} + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} \)  (Ampère–Maxwell Law)

These equations describe how electric and magnetic fields interact and propagate through space as electromagnetic waves.

2. Electromagnetic Waves

Maxwell’s equations predict that changing electric and magnetic fields generate waves traveling at the speed of light:

\( c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}} \)

These waves are the foundation of light, radio, X-rays, and all electromagnetic radiation.

3. Poynting Vector

The Poynting vector represents the power per unit area carried by electromagnetic waves:

\( \vec{S} = \frac{1}{\mu_0} (\vec{E} \times \vec{B}) \)

4. Energy Density

The total energy stored in electric and magnetic fields is given by:

\( u = \frac{1}{2}(\varepsilon_0 E^2 + \frac{B^2}{\mu_0}) \)

5. Applications

  • Wireless communication (radio, Wi-Fi, mobile networks)
  • Microwave ovens use electromagnetic waves for heating.
  • Optics and lasers are based on controlled EM radiation.
  • Transformers and inductors rely on magnetic induction.

Quantum Physics

Exploring the behavior of matter and energy at the smallest scales of nature.

1. Wave–Particle Duality

Light and matter exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties. Electrons can show interference patterns, while photons can eject electrons from metals.

\( E = hf \)     (Planck’s Relation)
\( \lambda = \frac{h}{p} \)     (de Broglie Wavelength)

Here, \( h \) is Planck’s constant, \( f \) is frequency, and \( p \) is momentum.

2. The Schrödinger Equation

The fundamental equation governing quantum systems:

\( i\hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} = \hat{H}\Psi \)

It describes how the quantum state \( \Psi \) evolves over time. The square of the wave function’s magnitude, \( |\Psi|^2 \), gives the probability density.

3. Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

The more precisely we know a particle’s position, the less precisely we can know its momentum:

\( \Delta x \, \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2} \)

4. Quantum Tunneling

Particles can pass through potential barriers that they classically shouldn’t overcome, a phenomenon used in diodes and nuclear fusion.

5. Applications

  • Quantum computing — uses qubits for immense computational power.
  • Semiconductors — based on quantum mechanics of electrons in solids.
  • Lasers — rely on stimulated emission and quantum transitions.
  • Quantum cryptography — secure communication using entanglement.

Nuclear Physics

Exploring the structure, forces, and reactions within atomic nuclei.

1. Nuclear Composition

A nucleus is composed of protons and neutrons bound by the strong nuclear force. Its stability depends on the balance between attractive nuclear and repulsive Coulomb forces.

2. Radioactive Decay

Unstable nuclei transform into more stable ones by emitting particles or radiation.

  • Alpha decay: \( ^A_ZX \rightarrow ^{A-4}_{Z-2}Y + ^4_2He \)
  • Beta decay: \( n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e \)
  • Gamma decay: \( ^A_ZX^* \rightarrow ^A_ZX + \gamma \)

3. Half-Life

\( N = N_0 e^{-\lambda t} \)

Where \( N_0 \) is the initial number of nuclei and \( \lambda \) is the decay constant.

4. Nuclear Reactions

Fission: A heavy nucleus splits into smaller nuclei, releasing energy.

Fusion: Light nuclei combine to form heavier nuclei, releasing even more energy.

\( E = \Delta m c^2 \)

5. Applications

  • Nuclear power — fission reactors generate electricity.
  • Medical imaging — PET and MRI use nuclear principles.
  • Radiocarbon dating — estimates age of ancient materials.
  • Fusion research — aims to produce clean, limitless energy.

Statistical Physics

Connecting microscopic states to macroscopic thermodynamics through probability.

1. Microstates and Macrostates

A system’s microstate is its exact microscopic configuration. A macrostate describes the observable quantities like pressure or temperature.

\( \Omega = \text{number of microstates corresponding to a macrostate} \)

2. Entropy

Entropy measures the disorder or multiplicity of a system:

\( S = k_B \ln \Omega \)

3. Ensembles

  • Microcanonical: fixed energy, volume, and particle number.
  • Canonical: fixed temperature and volume.
  • Grand Canonical: fixed temperature and chemical potential.

4. Partition Function

\( Z = \sum_i e^{-E_i / k_B T} \)

The partition function links microscopic energy levels \( E_i \) to macroscopic quantities:

\( F = -k_B T \ln Z \quad \text{and} \quad U = -\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta} \)

5. Boltzmann Distribution

\( P(E) = \frac{e^{-E / k_B T}}{Z} \)

This gives the probability that a system occupies a state with energy \( E \).

6. Applications

  • Understanding phase transitions and heat capacities.
  • Modeling gases, magnets, and quantum systems.
  • Foundation for quantum statistical mechanics.

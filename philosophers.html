Philosophers — From Ancient to Today

Chronological grouping of major philosophers across eras and traditions. Search, filter by era, or browse.

Ancient Philosophy (Before ~500 CE) Eastern & Western

Foundational thinkers from China, India, Greece, and Rome.
C
Confucius (551–479 BCE)
Chinese — Founder of Confucianism; ethics & social harmony.
L
Laozi (6th c. BCE?)
Chinese — Founder of Daoism; author of Tao Te Ching.
Z
Zhuangzi (4th c. BCE)
Chinese — Daoist philosopher emphasizing spontaneity and naturalness.
M
Mozi (470–391 BCE)
Chinese — Founder of Mohism; advocated universal love and pragmatic governance.
B
Buddha (Siddhartha Gautama) (c. 563–c. 483 BCE)
Indian — Founder of Buddhism; teachings on suffering, attachment, and the path to liberation.
M
Mahavira (599–527 BCE)
Indian — Key figure in Jain philosophy; emphasis on non-violence (Ahimsa).
C
Chanakya (Kautilya) (4th c. BCE)
Indian — Political philosopher; attributed author of the Arthashastra.
N
Nagarjuna (c. 150–250 CE)
Indian — Buddhist philosopher; founder of Madhyamaka (Middle Way) school.
T
Thales of Miletus (c. 624–546 BCE)
Greek — Often considered the first Greek philosopher; naturalist explanations for the world.
P
Pythagoras (c. 570–495 BCE)
Greek — Mathematics, metaphysics, and a religious-philosophical community focused on harmony.
H
Heraclitus (c. 535–475 BCE)
Greek — Doctrine of change: "everything flows"; focus on logos.
P
Parmenides (early 5th c. BCE)
Greek — Argued that being is unchanging and denounced the reality of change.
S
Socrates (469–399 BCE)
Greek — Ethics, dialectic method (Socratic questioning); left no writings.
P
Plato (427–347 BCE)
Greek — Theory of Forms; author of The Republic and many dialogues.
A
Aristotle (384–322 BCE)
Greek — Systematic philosopher: logic, metaphysics, ethics, natural science.
E
Epicurus (341–270 BCE)
Greek — Materialist ethics: pleasure as the absence of pain; simple living.
Z
Zeno of Citium (334–262 BCE)
Greek — Founder of Stoicism; virtue as the highest good.
P
Pyrrho (c. 360–270 BCE)
Greek — Early skeptic; suspension of judgment (epoché).
P
Plotinus (204–270 CE)
Neoplatonism — Metaphysical philosopher; emanation of the One.
C
Cicero (106–43 BCE)
Roman — Statesman and philosopher; transmitted Greek thought to Rome.
S
Seneca (4 BCE–65 CE)
Roman — Stoic philosopher, dramatist, and advisor to Nero.
E
Epictetus (50–135 CE)
Stoic — Former slave; taught freedom through inner discipline.
M
Marcus Aurelius (121–180 CE)
Roman — Stoic emperor; author of Meditations.

Medieval Philosophy (500–1500 CE)

Scholastic, Islamic, Jewish, and Asian thinkers who integrated religion and reason.
A
Augustine of Hippo (354–430)
Christian theologian — Synthesis of Christianity and Neoplatonism.
B
Boethius (480–524)
Philosopher — Author of The Consolation of Philosophy.
T
Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274)
Scholastic philosopher — Synthesized Aristotelianism and Christian theology.
A
Anselm of Canterbury (1033–1109)
Theologian — Famous for the ontological argument for God’s existence.
W
William of Ockham (c.1287–1347)
Philosopher — Known for Ockham's Razor: prefer simpler explanations.
A
Al-Farabi (872–950)
Islamic philosopher — Work on logic and political philosophy.
I
Avicenna (Ibn Sina) (980–1037)
Persian polymath — Influential in metaphysics, medicine, and Aristotelianism.
G
Al-Ghazali (1058–1111)
Islamic theologian — Critiqued philosophers in The Incoherence of the Philosophers.
A
Averroes (Ibn Rushd) (1126–1198)
Islamic philosopher — Major commentator on Aristotle.
I
Ibn Khaldun (1332–1406)
Historian & philosopher — Early thinker on sociology and cyclical history.
P
Philo of Alexandria (20 BCE–50 CE)
Jewish Hellenistic philosopher — Allegorical readings of scripture influenced later thought.
M
Maimonides (Moses ben Maimon) (1138–1204)
Jewish philosopher — Sought to reconcile faith with Aristotelian philosophy.
S
Adi Shankara (8th c.)
Indian — Proponent of Advaita Vedanta (non-dualism).
R
Ramanuja (1017–1137)
Indian — Theistic Vedanta; devotional emphasis.
Z
Zhu Xi (1130–1200)
Chinese — Leading Neo-Confucian synthesizer whose commentaries shaped East Asian thought.

Renaissance & Early Modern (1500–1800)

Thinkers who set the stage for modern science, politics, and epistemology.
N
Niccolò Machiavelli (1469–1527)
Political realist — Author of The Prince.
F
Francis Bacon (1561–1626)
Advocate of the scientific method and inductive reasoning.
R
René Descartes (1596–1650)
Rationalist philosopher — "Cogito, ergo sum"; foundationalism in epistemology.
T
Thomas Hobbes (1588–1679)
Political philosopher — Social contract theory in Leviathan.
B
Baruch Spinoza (1632–1677)
Ethics and metaphysics — A pantheistic vision of God and nature.
J
John Locke (1632–1704)
Empiricist — Political liberalism; rights and government by consent.
G
Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716)
Metaphysician — Monadology and rationalist contributions to math and logic.
G
George Berkeley (1685–1753)
Philosopher — Immaterialism/idealism: "to be is to be perceived."
D
David Hume (1711–1776)
Empiricist and skeptic — Critique of causation and induction.
R
Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778)
Political philosopher — Social contract, education, and natural freedom.
I
Immanuel Kant (1724–1804)
Critical philosophy — Epistemology and ethics: duty, autonomy, and transcendental idealism.

19th Century (1800–1900)

Philosophers wrestling with modernity, industrialization, and new social theories.
G
Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel (1770–1831)
Philosopher — Dialectical method and absolute idealism.
A
Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860)
Pessimistic metaphysics — Will as the fundamental reality.
J
John Stuart Mill (1806–1873)
Utilitarianism and liberal thought — On liberty and utilitarian ethics.
K
Karl Marx (1818–1883)
Social and political theorist — Historical materialism and critique of capitalism.
F
Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900)
Cultural critic — Will to power, critique of morality, existential themes.
S
Søren Kierkegaard (1813–1855)
Christian existentialism — Individual faith and subjectivity.
A
Auguste Comte (1798–1857)
Founder of positivism and early sociology.
H
Herbert Spencer (1820–1903)
Philosopher of social evolution — Associated with Social Darwinism.
W
William James (1842–1910)
Pragmatist philosopher — Contributions to psychology and philosophy of religion.

20th & 21st Century

Analytic, continental, political, feminist, and post-structuralist thinkers shaping contemporary debate.
B
Bertrand Russell (1872–1970)
Logic and analytic philosophy — Work in logic and philosophy of language.
L
Ludwig Wittgenstein (1889–1951)
Philosopher of language and mind — Tractatus and later Philosophical Investigations.
M
Martin Heidegger (1889–1976)
Existential phenomenology — Being and time; ontology and critique of modernity.
J
Jean-Paul Sartre (1905–1980)
Existentialist — Emphasis on radical freedom and responsibility.
S
Simone de Beauvoir (1908–1986)
Feminist existentialism — Author of The Second Sex.
H
Hannah Arendt (1906–1975)
Political theorist — Work on totalitarianism, authority, and the public realm.
A
Albert Camus (1913–1960)
Philosopher & writer — Associated with absurdism and revolt.
M
Michel Foucault (1926–1984)
Post-structuralist — Studies of power, institutions, and knowledge.
J
Jacques Derrida (1930–2004)
Founder of deconstruction — Rethinking text, meaning, and binary oppositions.
J
Jürgen Habermas (1929– )
Critical theorist — Communicative action and deliberative democracy.
N
Noam Chomsky (1928– )
Linguist & political theorist — Generative grammar and political critique.
S
Slavoj Žižek (1949– )
Contemporary theorist — Psychoanalytic-Marxist cultural critique.
J
Judith Butler (1956– )
Gender theorist — Notions of performativity and social construction of gender.
P
Peter Singer (1946– )
Ethicist — Practical ethics, animal liberation, utilitarian arguments.
M
Martha Nussbaum (1947– )
Ethics and political philosophy — Capabilities approach and human development.

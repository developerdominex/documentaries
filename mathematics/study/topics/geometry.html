Geometry

The study of shapes, sizes, positions, and dimensions of figures and spaces.

Back to Topics

1. Introduction

Geometry is the branch of mathematics that deals with the properties and relationships of points, lines, surfaces, and solids. It provides a way to measure, design, and understand space and form — both abstractly and in the physical world.

Historical Background

Geometry originated in ancient Egypt and Babylon for land measurement and construction. The Greek mathematician Euclid (c. 300 BCE) organized known geometric facts into a logical system, resulting in his famous book, Elements — a foundation of mathematics for over 2,000 years.

2. Basic Elements

  • Point: A location with no size or dimension.
  • Line: A straight path extending infinitely in both directions.
  • Line Segment: A part of a line with two endpoints.
  • Ray: A part of a line that starts at one point and extends infinitely in one direction.
  • Plane: A flat surface extending infinitely in all directions.

3. Angles

An angle is formed when two rays share a common endpoint (called the vertex).

  • Acute Angle — less than 90°
  • Right Angle — exactly 90°
  • Obtuse Angle — between 90° and 180°
  • Straight Angle — exactly 180°
  • Reflex Angle — between 180° and 360°

Example: If two lines intersect forming one 90° angle, all four angles are right angles.

4. Triangles

Triangles are polygons with three sides. They are classified by sides and angles:

By Sides:

  • Equilateral — all sides equal
  • Isosceles — two sides equal
  • Scalene — no sides equal

By Angles:

  • Acute Triangle — all angles < 90°
  • Right Triangle — one 90° angle
  • Obtuse Triangle — one angle > 90°

Triangle Properties

  • Sum of interior angles = 180°
  • Pythagoras’ Theorem (Right Triangle): a² + b² = c²
  • Area = ½ × base × height
  • Perimeter = sum of all sides

Example: A right triangle with sides 3 and 4 → hypotenuse = √(3² + 4²) = 5.

5. Quadrilaterals

  • Square — all sides equal, all angles 90°
  • Rectangle — opposite sides equal, all angles 90°
  • Parallelogram — opposite sides and angles equal
  • Rhombus — all sides equal, opposite angles equal
  • Trapezium — one pair of parallel sides

Example: Area of rectangle = length × width

6. Circles

  • Radius (r): Distance from center to any point on the circle
  • Diameter (d): 2 × radius
  • Circumference (C): 2πr
  • Area (A): πr²

Example: A circle with radius 7 cm → Area = π(7²) = 49π ≈ 153.94 cm²

7. Polygons

Polygons are closed figures with three or more straight sides.

  • Triangle — 3 sides
  • Quadrilateral — 4 sides
  • Pentagon — 5 sides
  • Hexagon — 6 sides
  • Heptagon — 7 sides
  • Octagon — 8 sides

Sum of interior angles = (n − 2) × 180°

Each interior angle (regular polygon) = [(n − 2) × 180°] / n

8. Coordinate Geometry

Coordinate geometry combines algebra and geometry. Points are represented by ordered pairs (x, y) on a Cartesian plane.

Distance between points (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂):

√[(x₂ − x₁)² + (y₂ − y₁)²]

Midpoint: ((x₁ + x₂)/2, (y₁ + y₂)/2)

9. Solid Geometry

Solid geometry studies 3D shapes — objects having length, breadth, and height.

  • Cube — Volume = a³; Surface Area = 6a²
  • Cuboid — Volume = l × b × h
  • Cylinder — Volume = πr²h; Surface Area = 2πr(h + r)
  • Cone — Volume = (1/3)πr²h
  • Sphere — Volume = (4/3)πr³; Surface Area = 4πr²

10. Theorems & Proofs

  • Angles on a straight line = 180°
  • Angles at a point = 360°
  • Opposite angles of a parallelogram are equal
  • Base angles of an isosceles triangle are equal
  • Sum of interior angles of any triangle = 180°

11. Modern Applications

  • Architecture and Engineering design
  • Computer graphics and 3D modeling
  • Navigation and GPS systems
  • Physics, Astronomy, Robotics

12. Summary

Geometry builds spatial reasoning and problem-solving skills. It connects visual thinking to logic — forming the bridge between mathematics and the physical world.

Probability & Statistics

Random variables, distributions, and stochastic processes

Introduction

Probability theory studies the likelihood of events occurring. Statistics deals with data collection, analysis, interpretation, and presentation. Together, they provide tools to model uncertainty and variability.

Probability Basics

  • Sample space: Set of all possible outcomes, e.g., S = {1,2,3,4,5,6} for a die.
  • Event: Subset of sample space, e.g., rolling an even number E={2,4,6}.
  • Probability of event: P(E) = Number of favorable outcomes / Total outcomes.
Example: Probability of rolling a 4 on a die: P({4}) = 1/6

Random Variables

A random variable assigns a number to each outcome of a random experiment.

  • Discrete: takes finite or countable values, e.g., X = number of heads in 3 coin tosses.
  • Continuous: takes values in an interval, e.g., Y = height of students in a class.
Example: X = number of 6’s in 2 dice rolls: X ∈ {0,1,2}, P(X=1)=2/3

Distributions

  • Discrete: Binomial, Poisson, Geometric
  • Continuous: Uniform, Normal, Exponential

Stochastic Processes

A stochastic process is a collection of random variables indexed by time or space. Examples include:

  • Markov chains
  • Poisson process
  • Brownian motion

Applications

  • Finance: modeling stock prices and risk
  • Engineering: reliability and queuing theory
  • Computer science: algorithms, machine learning
  • Science: population modeling, genetics

History

Probability theory began with gambling problems studied by Pascal and Fermat. Modern probability and statistics were developed by Laplace, Gauss, and Kolmogorov.

Arithmetic

The foundation of all mathematics — the science of numbers and operations.

1. Introduction

Arithmetic is the oldest and most fundamental branch of mathematics, dealing with numbers and the basic operations performed on them: addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. It forms the core of number theory, algebra, and analysis.

Definition

Arithmetic is the study of numbers, properties of numbers, and computations involving them. It includes natural numbers, integers, rational numbers, real numbers, and sometimes complex numbers.

2. History of Arithmetic

The history of arithmetic goes back to the dawn of civilization. Early humans used counting stones and tally marks. Around 3000 BCE, the Sumerians and Egyptians developed symbols for numbers. The Babylonians used a base-60 system, and the Greeks later formalized arithmetic as a mathematical discipline.

The most important advance came from the Hindu–Arabic numeral system, developed in India and transmitted to the Islamic world and Europe. This system (0–9) made modern arithmetic and algorithms possible.

3. Number Systems

  • Natural Numbers (ℕ): 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, …
  • Whole Numbers: 0, 1, 2, 3, …
  • Integers (ℤ): …, -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, …
  • Rational Numbers (ℚ): Numbers that can be expressed as p/q.
  • Irrational Numbers: Cannot be written as fractions (e.g., √2, π).
  • Real Numbers (ℝ): All rational and irrational numbers.
  • Complex Numbers (ℂ): Numbers of the form a + bi, where i² = -1.

4. Basic Operations

Addition (+)

Combining two or more quantities. Example: 7 + 5 = 12

Subtraction (−)

Finding the difference between two numbers. Example: 9 − 4 = 5

Multiplication (×)

Repeated addition. Example: 3 × 4 = 12

Division (÷)

Sharing or grouping equally. Example: 20 ÷ 5 = 4

5. Properties of Operations

  • Commutative Law: a + b = b + a, a × b = b × a
  • Associative Law: (a + b) + c = a + (b + c)
  • Distributive Law: a × (b + c) = a×b + a×c
  • Identity Elements: a + 0 = a; a × 1 = a
  • Inverse Elements: a + (−a) = 0; a × (1/a) = 1 (a ≠ 0)

6. Factors, Multiples & Divisibility

- A factor of a number divides it exactly. - A multiple is a number obtained by multiplying it by an integer. - Divisibility rules help to check if one number divides another without actual division.

Examples:

  • 6 is divisible by 2 and 3.
  • Factors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12.
  • Multiples of 5: 5, 10, 15, 20, …

7. Fractions, Decimals, and Percentages

Fractions

Part of a whole, expressed as numerator/denominator.

Example: ¾ means 3 parts out of 4 equal parts.

Decimals

Fractions written using base 10 notation. ½ = 0.5

Percentages

Percent means "per hundred". 25% = 25/100 = 0.25

8. Ratios and Proportions

A ratio compares two quantities: Ratio of a to b = a : b

A proportion states that two ratios are equal: a/b = c/d

Example: If 2 pencils cost $4, then 4 pencils cost $8. The ratio 2:4 = 4:8 shows a proportion.

9. Order of Operations (BODMAS)

The correct order for solving expressions is: Brackets → Orders (powers, roots) → Division → Multiplication → Addition → Subtraction.

Example: 8 + 2 × (3 + 1)² = 8 + 2 × 16 = 8 + 32 = 40

10. Real-World Applications

  • Finance — interest, profit and loss, tax, discounts
  • Measurements — length, area, volume, time, speed
  • Data — averages, proportions, and scaling

11. Modern Extensions

Arithmetic extends to computational systems, modular arithmetic in cryptography, and algorithms in computer science. It’s the foundation of programming and logical computation.

12. Summary

Arithmetic is not merely calculation — it’s a language of quantity and logic. From ancient counting to quantum computing, arithmetic remains the heart of mathematics.

Differentiation

Derivatives, limits, continuity, tangent slopes, and applications.

Introduction to Differentiation

Differentiation is the process of finding the derivative of a function, which measures how the function changes as its input changes. Geometrically, the derivative represents the slope of the tangent to the curve at a point.

Example: f(x) = x² → f'(x) = 2x

Limits

The derivative is defined using limits:

f'(x) = lim(h→0) [f(x+h) - f(x)] / h
f(x) = x² → f'(x) = lim(h→0) [(x+h)² - x²]/h = lim(h→0) [2xh + h²]/h = 2x

Continuity

A function must be continuous at a point to be differentiable there. Continuous means no jumps, breaks, or holes in the function.

f(x) = |x| → Not differentiable at x = 0 (corner)

Rules of Differentiation

  • Constant rule: (c)' = 0
  • Power rule: (xⁿ)' = n·xⁿ⁻¹
  • Sum/Difference: (f ± g)' = f' ± g'
  • Product rule: (fg)' = f'g + fg'
  • Quotient rule: (f/g)' = (f'g - fg')/g²
  • Chain rule: (f(g(x)))' = f'(g(x)) · g'(x)
f(x) = x²·sin(x) → f'(x) = 2x·sin(x) + x²·cos(x) (Product rule)

Higher-Order Derivatives

Second derivative f''(x) measures the curvature of the graph. Higher derivatives are obtained by differentiating repeatedly.

f(x) = x³ → f'(x) = 3x², f''(x) = 6x, f'''(x) = 6

Tangent & Normal

The slope of tangent at x = a is f'(a). Normal is perpendicular to tangent.

Applications

  • Finding maxima and minima of functions
  • Velocity and acceleration in physics
  • Approximation of functions (linearization)
  • Engineering optimization problems
  • Curve sketching and analysis

History

Differentiation as a systematic study began in the 17th century with Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz. Newton focused on rates of change, while Leibniz developed the symbolic notation that we use today.

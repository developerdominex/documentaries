Abstract Algebra

Groups, rings, fields, and Galois theory

Introduction

Abstract algebra studies algebraic structures such as groups, rings, fields, and their generalizations. It provides a framework for understanding symmetry, number systems, and mathematical operations at a structural level.

Groups

A group \( (G, *) \) is a set with a binary operation satisfying:

  • Closure: \( a*b \in G \)
  • Associativity: \( (a*b)*c = a*(b*c) \)
  • Identity: \( \exists e \in G: e*a = a*e = a \)
  • Inverse: \( \forall a \in G, \exists a^{-1}: a*a^{-1} = e \)
Example: Integers under addition \( (\mathbb{Z}, +) \) form a group.

Rings

A ring \( (R, +, \cdot) \) is a set with two operations: addition (forming an abelian group) and multiplication (associative), with distributive laws:

\[ a(b+c) = ab + ac, \quad (a+b)c = ac + bc \]
Example: Integers \( (\mathbb{Z}, +, \cdot) \) form a ring.

Fields

A field \( (F, +, \cdot) \) is a ring where every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse, and multiplication is commutative.

Example: Rational numbers \( (\mathbb{Q}, +, \cdot) \), real numbers \( (\mathbb{R}, +, \cdot) \)

Subgroups and Homomorphisms

  • Subgroup: a subset \( H \subset G \) that forms a group under the same operation
  • Homomorphism: a map \( f: G \to H \) such that \( f(a*b) = f(a)*f(b) \)
  • Isomorphism: bijective homomorphism, indicates structural similarity

Galois Theory

Galois theory connects field theory and group theory to study solvability of polynomials. Key ideas:

  • Galois group: automorphisms of a field extension preserving base field
  • Solvable groups correspond to solvable polynomials by radicals
  • Fundamental theorem of Galois theory: bijection between subgroups of Galois group and intermediate fields
Example: The polynomial \( x^5 - x - 1 = 0 \) is not solvable by radicals because its Galois group is not solvable.

Applications

  • Cryptography (RSA, ECC)
  • Coding theory
  • Symmetry in chemistry and physics
  • Solving polynomial equations and advanced algebra

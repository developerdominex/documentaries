Unsolved Problems in Mathematics

Riemann Hypothesis, P vs NP, and Millennium Problems

Introduction

Despite centuries of study, there remain fundamental problems in mathematics that are still unsolved. These problems often drive research and inspire new theories.

Millennium Prize Problems

In 2000, the Clay Mathematics Institute announced seven Millennium Prize Problems, each with a reward of $1 million for a solution:

  • P vs NP Problem: Is every problem whose solution can be quickly verified also quickly solvable?
  • Riemann Hypothesis: All nontrivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function have real part 1/2.
  • Yang–Mills Existence and Mass Gap: Prove existence of a quantum Yang–Mills theory and a mass gap.
  • Navier–Stokes Existence and Smoothness: Existence and smoothness of solutions in 3D fluid flow equations.
  • Hodge Conjecture: Relates algebraic cycles to cohomology classes of projective varieties.
  • Poincaré Conjecture: Already solved by Grigori Perelman (2003).
  • Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer Conjecture: Predicts the rank of elliptic curves from their L-functions.

Other Famous Unsolved Problems

  • Goldbach Conjecture: Every even integer greater than 2 is the sum of two primes.
  • Collatz Conjecture: Iterating the sequence n → n/2 if even, 3n+1 if odd, eventually reaches 1 for all n.
  • Twin Prime Conjecture: There are infinitely many primes p such that p+2 is also prime.
  • Euler’s Sum of Powers Conjecture: Counterexamples found, but higher powers remain challenging.
  • Continuum Hypothesis: No set with cardinality strictly between integers and real numbers (undecidable in ZFC).

Why They Matter

Solving these problems can:

  • Advance mathematical theory
  • Improve computational techniques
  • Provide insight into physics, computer science, and cryptography
  • Inspire generations of mathematicians

Linear Algebra

Vector spaces, matrices, eigenvalues, transformations, and diagonalization

Introduction

Linear algebra is the branch of mathematics concerning vector spaces and linear mappings between them. It studies systems of linear equations, matrices, determinants, eigenvalues, and transformations.

Vector Spaces

  • A set of vectors with operations of addition and scalar multiplication
  • Closed under addition and scalar multiplication
  • Contains a zero vector \( \mathbf{0} \)
Example: \( \mathbb{R}^3 = \{(x,y,z) | x,y,z \in \mathbb{R}\} \)

Matrices and Determinants

Matrices represent linear transformations. Determinants provide information about invertibility.

\( A = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 2\\ 3 & 4\end{bmatrix}, \quad \det(A) = (1)(4) - (2)(3) = -2 \)

2x2 matrix visualization

Linear Transformations

A linear transformation \( T: V \to W \) preserves addition and scalar multiplication:

\[ T(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = T(\mathbf{u}) + T(\mathbf{v}), \quad T(c\mathbf{u}) = c T(\mathbf{u}) \]
\( T(x,y) = (2x, 3y) \)

Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

For a square matrix \( A \), a vector \( v \neq 0 \) is an eigenvector if \( A v = \lambda v \), where \( \lambda \) is the eigenvalue.

\( A = \begin{bmatrix}2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3\end{bmatrix} \) → Eigenvalues: \( \lambda_1 = 2, \lambda_2 = 3 \)
Eigenvectors: \( v_1 = \begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix}, v_2 = \begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix} \)

Diagonalization

A matrix \( A \) is diagonalizable if there exists an invertible matrix \( P \) such that \( P^{-1}AP = D \), where \( D \) is diagonal with eigenvalues on the diagonal.

\( A = \begin{bmatrix}4 & 1 \\ 0 & 2\end{bmatrix} \) → Diagonalizable with eigenvalues 4 and 2

Applications

  • Computer graphics: transformations, rotations
  • Engineering: systems of linear equations, stress analysis
  • Economics: input-output models, optimization
  • Data science: PCA, dimensionality reduction

History

Linear algebra emerged in the 19th century, building on matrix theory by mathematicians like Arthur Cayley and James Joseph Sylvester. It is fundamental in modern mathematics, physics, and engineering.

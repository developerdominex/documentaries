Mensuration

Perimeter, area, surface area, and volume — measurement of shapes and solids.

1. Introduction

Mensuration is the branch of mathematics that deals with measuring geometric figures: lengths, areas and volumes. It provides formulas to compute perimeter, area of plane figures, surface area and volume of solids. Applications include architecture, engineering, surveying, physics and everyday measurement tasks.

Historical note

Practical mensuration dates back to ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia where surveyors measured land and volumes for construction. The Greeks developed rigorous geometry; modern formulaic mensuration evolved through classical and medieval mathematics and was formalized in textbooks for engineering and architecture.

2. Perimeter and Circumference

The perimeter of a polygon is the total length around it. For a circle the equivalent is the circumference.

Perimeter (polygon) = sum of side lengths
Circumference of circle = 2πr = πd

Examples

Rectangle with length 8 and width 5 → Perimeter = 2(8 + 5) = 26.

Circle with radius 7 → Circumference = 2π×7 ≈ 43.98.

3. Areas of Plane Figures

Formulas for common shapes are listed and illustrated. Diagrams are created using inline SVG so you can see geometry visually.

Rectangle

Area = length × width = l × w

Square

Area = side² = a²

Triangle

Area = ½ × base × height = (1/2) b h

Parallelogram

Area = base × height = b × h

Trapezium (Trapezoid)

Area = ½ × (sum of parallel sides) × height = ½ (a + b) h

Circle

Area = π r²

Area summary (quick)

Rectangle: A = l × w
Square: A = a²
Triangle: A = ½ b h
Circle: A = π r²
Trapezium: A = ½ (a + b) h

4. Surface Area of 3D Solids

Surface area measures the total area covered by the surface of a solid.

Cube

Surface area = 6 a² (a = side)

Cuboid (Rectangular Prism)

Surface area = 2(lw + lh + wh)

Cylinder

Surface area (total) = 2πr(h + r) [lateral + 2 circular bases]

Sphere

Surface area = 4πr²

5. Volumes of Solids

The volume is the measure of space occupied by a solid.

Cube: V = a³
Cuboid: V = l × w × h
Cylinder: V = π r² h
Cone: V = (1/3) π r² h
Sphere: V = (4/3) π r³

6. Composite Shapes and Decomposition

Complex areas or volumes can be found by decomposing a figure into simpler parts (rectangles, triangles, circles), computing each area/volume, and combining them (adding or subtracting).

Example: Area of an L-shaped figure = area of two rectangles (compute separately and add).

7. Practical Examples

1. Flooring: Room 5.2 m by 3.6 m → floor area = 5.2 × 3.6 = 18.72 m².

2. Paint for walls: A wall 4 m × 3 m with one window 1.2 m × 1.0 m → paint area = (4×3) − (1.2×1.0) = 12 − 1.2 = 10.8 m².

3. Water in a cylindrical tank: Tank r = 1.5 m, h = 4 m → Volume = π(1.5)²×4 ≈ 28.274 m³.

8. Tips and Common Mistakes

  • Always check units — convert to same units before computing area or volume.
  • Use π ≈ 3.14159 or exact π for symbolic answers; be consistent.
  • For composite figures, sketch and label each part before computation.
  • Remember surface area includes all exposed faces; lateral area excludes bases for cylinders/cones.

9. Exercises (Practice)

  1. Find area of a triangle with base 12 cm and height 5 cm.
  2. Compute the surface area and volume of a cube of side 8 cm.
  3. A right circular cone has r = 3 cm and slant height l = 5 cm. Find lateral surface area and volume.
  4. Calculate the area between two concentric circles with radii 10 cm and 6 cm.

Answers: 1) 30 cm². 2) SA=6×64=384 cm², V=512 cm³. 3) Lateral area=πr l = 15π cm², V=(1/3)π r² h (need h). 4) Area difference = π(100−36)=64π cm².

Why mensuration matters

From calculating material quantities to designing volumes and capacities, mensuration converts geometric thinking into actionable measurements for construction, manufacturing and science.

Topology

Open sets, continuity, homotopy, and fundamental groups

Introduction

Topology is the study of geometric properties preserved under continuous deformations, such as stretching or bending, but not tearing or gluing. It generalizes concepts of geometry to more abstract spaces.

Open Sets and Topologies

A topology on a set X is a collection of subsets (called open sets) that satisfies:

  • The empty set ∅ and X are open
  • Arbitrary unions of open sets are open
  • Finite intersections of open sets are open
Example: In ℝ, open intervals (a,b) form the standard topology.

Continuity

A function \( f: X \to Y \) between topological spaces is continuous if the preimage of every open set in Y is open in X.

Continuous functions in ℝ correspond to the usual ε-δ definition from calculus.

Homeomorphisms

A homeomorphism is a bijective continuous function with a continuous inverse. Two spaces are topologically equivalent if there exists a homeomorphism between them.

Homotopy

Two continuous functions f, g: X → Y are homotopic if one can be continuously deformed into the other.

Example: A circle can be continuously deformed into a square in the plane.

Fundamental Group

The fundamental group π₁(X, x₀) captures information about loops in space X based at point x₀.

  • Loop: path starting and ending at x₀
  • Two loops are equivalent if one can be continuously deformed into the other (homotopy)
  • Group operation: concatenation of loops
Example: π₁(S¹) ≅ ℤ, the integers under addition (number of times a loop winds around the circle)

Applications

  • Analysis of shapes in mathematics and computer graphics
  • Network topology in computer science
  • Algebraic topology for classifying surfaces
  • Robotics and motion planning

History

Topology emerged in the 19th century with contributions from Euler (bridges of Königsberg), Riemann, Poincaré, and others. Modern algebraic topology was formalized in the early 20th century.

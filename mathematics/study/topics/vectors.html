Vectors

Vector algebra, dot and cross product, 3D geometry.

Introduction to Vectors

A vector is a quantity that has both magnitude and direction. It is usually represented as v = ai + bj + ck in 3D or v = ai + bj in 2D.

Example: v = 3i + 4j → Magnitude = √(3² + 4²) = 5

Vector Algebra

  • Addition: u + v = (u₁+v₁)i + (u₂+v₂)j (+ (u₃+v₃)k)
  • Subtraction: u - v = (u₁-v₁)i + (u₂-v₂)j (+ (u₃-v₃)k)
  • Scalar Multiplication: k·v = k(v₁i + v₂j + v₃k)
u = 2i + 3j, v = i + 4j → u + v = 3i + 7j

Dot Product

The dot product of two vectors u and v is u·v = |u||v|cosθ or u·v = u₁v₁ + u₂v₂ (+ u₃v₃).

u = 2i + 3j, v = i + 4j → u·v = 2*1 + 3*4 = 14

Cross Product

The cross product of two vectors u and v is a vector perpendicular to both, with magnitude |u × v| = |u||v|sinθ and direction determined by the right-hand rule.

u = i + j, v = j + k → u × v = |i j k|
|1 1 0|
|0 1 1| = i - j + k

3D Vectors & Geometry

Vectors in 3D space are represented as v = ai + bj + ck. They can define lines, planes, and compute angles between vectors.

Line: r = r₀ + t·v
Plane: ax + by + cz = d

Applications of Vectors

  • Physics: displacement, velocity, and force vectors
  • Engineering: 3D modeling and mechanics
  • Computer graphics and animation
  • Navigation and robotics

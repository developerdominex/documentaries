Number Theory

Primes, modular arithmetic, and Diophantine equations

Introduction

Number theory is the branch of mathematics concerned with properties and relationships of integers. It includes the study of prime numbers, divisibility, modular arithmetic, and solutions to equations with integer solutions.

Prime Numbers

A prime number is an integer greater than 1 that has no positive divisors other than 1 and itself.

Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17...

Fundamental theorem of arithmetic: Every integer greater than 1 is either prime or can be uniquely factored into primes.

Modular Arithmetic

Modular arithmetic deals with integers modulo n. Two integers a and b are congruent modulo n if n divides (a-b):

a ≡ b (mod n)

Example: 17 ≡ 5 (mod 12), because 12 divides (17-5)=12

Applications include cryptography (RSA), cyclic patterns, and solving congruences.

Diophantine Equations

Diophantine equations are polynomial equations with integer solutions.

Example: Find integers x, y such that 3x + 4y = 10. Solution: x=2, y=1

Famous problems: Fermat’s Last Theorem, Pell’s equation, and linear Diophantine equations.

Applications

  • Cryptography: RSA, Diffie-Hellman key exchange
  • Computer science: hashing, pseudorandom number generation
  • Pure mathematics: integer sequences, algebraic structures

History

Number theory dates back to ancient civilizations. Euclid studied prime numbers, Diophantus studied integer solutions to equations. Modern developments include contributions from Euler, Gauss, and Riemann.

Complex Analysis

Analytic functions, Cauchy’s theorem, and contour integration

Introduction

Complex analysis studies functions of a complex variable. It explores analytic functions, contour integration, singularities, and powerful theorems like Cauchy’s theorem and residue theory.

Complex Numbers Recap

  • Complex number: \( z = x + iy \), \( i^2 = -1 \)
  • Modulus: \( |z| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} \)
  • Argument: \( \arg(z) = \theta \) in polar form \( z = r e^{i\theta} \)

Analytic Functions

A function \( f(z) \) is analytic at \( z_0 \) if it is differentiable in a neighborhood of \( z_0 \).

\( f(z) = z^2 + 3z + 5 \) is analytic everywhere in \( \mathbb{C} \)

Cauchy-Riemann Equations

For \( f(z) = u(x,y) + iv(x,y) \), analytic iff:

\[ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}, \quad \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} \]
\( f(z) = x^2 - y^2 + i(2xy) \) satisfies Cauchy-Riemann equations → analytic

Contour Integration

Integration of complex functions along paths \( \gamma \) in the complex plane:

\[ \int_\gamma f(z) \, dz \]
Example: \( \int_{|z|=1} z^n dz = 0 \) for \( n \neq -1 \)

Contour around origin

Cauchy’s Theorem

If \( f(z) \) is analytic inside and on a simple closed contour \( C \), then:

\[ \oint_C f(z) dz = 0 \]
Useful for evaluating integrals and proving fundamental results in complex analysis.

Residue Theory

  • Residue of \( f(z) \) at singularity \( z_0 \): \( \text{Res}(f, z_0) \)
  • Integral around contour: \( \oint_C f(z) dz = 2\pi i \sum \text{Res}(f, z_k) \)
\( f(z) = \frac{1}{z^2+1} \) has residues \( \frac{i}{2}, -\frac{i}{2} \) at \( i \) and \( -i \)

Applications

  • Complex function theory in physics and engineering
  • Evaluation of real integrals using contour integration
  • Fluid dynamics, electrostatics, and conformal mapping
  • Signal processing and Fourier analysis

History

Complex analysis was developed in the 19th century by mathematicians like Augustin-Louis Cauchy, Bernhard Riemann, and Karl Weierstrass. It is a cornerstone of modern mathematical analysis.

Mole Concept and Avogadro’s Law

Understanding the quantity of substance and its relationship to particles and volume.

Introduction

The mole concept is central to quantitative chemistry, linking microscopic particles to macroscopic amounts of substances. Avogadro’s law provides a critical understanding of the behavior of gases and their relation to the number of particles.

Mole Concept

A mole is the amount of substance containing exactly 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro’s number). It allows chemists to count atoms, ions, and molecules in laboratory measurements.

  • Molar Mass: Mass of 1 mole of a substance (g/mol).
  • Number of Particles: n = N / NA, where N is the number of entities and NA is Avogadro's number.
  • Stoichiometry: Moles are used to calculate reactants and products in chemical reactions.

Avogadro’s Law

At constant temperature and pressure, equal volumes of gases contain an equal number of particles.

Mathematically: V ∝ n or V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂

This principle helps predict volumes of gases in reactions, understand molar volume (22.4 L at STP), and is foundational for ideal gas calculations.

Applications

  • Calculating amounts of reactants/products in chemical reactions.
  • Gas stoichiometry and determining molecular formulas.
  • Linking microscopic particle count to macroscopic measurements in labs.

Examples

1 mole of H₂ molecules contains 6.022 × 10²³ H₂ molecules and weighs 2 g. Similarly, 1 mole of NaCl contains 6.022 × 10²³ formula units and weighs 58.44 g.

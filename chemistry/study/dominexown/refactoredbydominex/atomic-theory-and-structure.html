Atomic Theory and Structure of Atom

Understanding the building blocks of matter and their internal structure.

Introduction

Atomic theory explains that all matter is composed of discrete units called atoms. Over centuries, models of the atom have evolved to reflect experimental discoveries and quantum mechanics.

Dalton’s Atomic Model

John Dalton proposed that atoms are indivisible, indestructible, and combine in fixed ratios to form compounds. This model laid the foundation of modern chemistry.

Thomson’s Model (Plum Pudding)

J.J. Thomson discovered the electron, suggesting atoms are spheres of positive charge with embedded electrons.

Rutherford Model

Ernest Rutherford’s gold foil experiment revealed that atoms have a dense nucleus with positive charge, surrounded by electrons in mostly empty space.

Bohr Model

Niels Bohr introduced quantized electron orbits, explaining hydrogen’s emission spectrum.

Quantum Mechanical Model

Modern atomic theory is based on Schrödinger’s wave equation, describing electron probability clouds. Electrons occupy orbitals (s, p, d, f), with energy levels and shapes determining chemical properties.

Subatomic Particles

  • Protons: Positive charge, in nucleus, determines element identity.
  • Neutrons: Neutral, in nucleus, contributes to isotope behavior.
  • Electrons: Negative charge, in orbitals, governs chemical reactivity.

Atomic Number and Mass Number

The atomic number (Z) is the number of protons in the nucleus, defining the element. Mass number (A) is the sum of protons and neutrons. Isotopes have same Z but different A.

Applications

Understanding atomic structure is essential for:

  • Predicting chemical bonding and reactivity.
  • Nuclear chemistry, radiation studies, and isotopes.
  • Quantum chemistry, spectroscopy, and material science.

⚛️ Atomic Models

Dalton’s Model (1803)

Dalton proposed that atoms are indivisible, indestructible spheres. Each element consists of identical atoms, and chemical reactions are rearrangements of these atoms.

Key Points:

  • Atoms are indivisible and indestructible
  • All atoms of an element are identical in mass and properties
  • Compounds are formed by combination of atoms in fixed ratios

Thomson’s Model (1897)

Known as the “plum pudding” model, Thomson proposed that atoms consist of a positively charged sphere with embedded electrons.

Key Points:

  • Atom is divisible
  • Positive “pudding” with negative electrons embedded
  • Disproved by Rutherford’s gold foil experiment later

Rutherford’s Model (1911)

Rutherford proposed that the atom has a dense positively charged nucleus and electrons orbit around it. The atom is mostly empty space.

Key Points:

  • Atom contains a central nucleus
  • Electrons orbit the nucleus
  • Disproved Thomson’s plum pudding model

Bohr Model (1913)

Bohr introduced quantized energy levels for electrons. Electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed shells without radiating energy.

Key Points:

  • Electrons move in fixed orbits (energy levels)
  • Energy is absorbed/emitted in quanta when electrons jump levels
  • Explains hydrogen emission spectra

Quantum Mechanical Model

Modern atomic theory uses wave mechanics. Electrons are described by wavefunctions and occupy probabilistic orbitals rather than fixed paths.

Key Points:

  • Electron position is probabilistic
  • Described by Schrödinger equation
  • Orbitals (s, p, d, f) define electron density regions
  • Explains chemical bonding and periodicity

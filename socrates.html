Socrates

The Father of Western Philosophy (469–399 BCE)

Socrates, one of the most profound thinkers of ancient Greece, transformed philosophy from abstract speculation to an examination of human ethics and self-knowledge. He left no written works of his own, but his student Plato immortalized his ideas and method of dialogue — the Socratic Method — in timeless dialogues.

A man of unmatched moral integrity, Socrates believed that wisdom begins in recognizing one’s ignorance. His relentless questioning challenged the assumptions of Athenian society, provoking admiration and controversy alike. In 399 BCE, he was condemned to death for "corrupting the youth" and "impiety", yet faced his fate with calm dignity.

“The unexamined life is not worth living.”

His death became a symbol of intellectual freedom and integrity. Socrates’ courage to stand by his principles, even unto death, laid the ethical foundation for Western thought — influencing philosophers from Plato and Aristotle to Descartes and Nietzsche.
